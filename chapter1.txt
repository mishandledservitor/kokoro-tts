The Squirrels of the Great Racetrack. A story by a very imaginative 5-year-old. A Note Before We Begin. This story was supposed to start with a freezer full of food — enough to feed every cat, hamster, bunny, and rodent in the entire world. The author would like you to know that. It is important. Now, the actual story. Chapter 1: The Racetrack. Once upon a time, there was a racetrack. Not just any racetrack — the greatest racetrack in the world, and it belonged to the squirrels. The squirrels were fast. Faster than you'd think. They zipped around the track every single day, and they were very proud of it. Living alongside the squirrels were all sorts of creatures: jellyfish, crabs, lobsters, shrimp, scorpions, and snakes. And cockroaches too, which the squirrels used to catch other bugs. Nobody asked too many questions about that. Their greatest ally was a spider — a hydraulic spider, all the way from India (though some said Albania). He was enormous. He was ancient. He was the biggest brother the squirrel school had ever known. And he could take on any bug that dared cause trouble. The squirrels had each other, and they had their racetrack, and that was enough. Chapter 2: Friends and Disguises. The squirrels were brave about most things, but there was one thing they never talked about: certain animals made them nervous. They were friendly with dogs — or at least they tried to be — but between you and me, they didn't actually like dogs very much. In fact, squirrels were the first creatures in all of history to figure out how to escape from dogs. Their secret? Disguises. The squirrel sister wore one disguise. The squirrel brother wore another. And with their costumes on, no dog could ever catch them. They made their homes in the trees — cosy little nests, doubled up to make them twice as warm. They were American squirrels, which meant they were awake in the sunshine and asleep by moonlight. But not every night was peaceful. Some nights brought fires and explosions. Waterspouts and lightning. Comets blazing overhead. Ice storms, downpours, and hurricanes that sent everyone diving for their umbrellas. And then — just when things seemed at their worst — the snow would come. Soft and quiet. And everything would be cosy again. On those cosy days, the squirrels feasted: corn, peppers, cucumbers, watermelons, lemons so sour they made your eyes water, and avocados. Always avocados. Chapter 3: Everything Else the Squirrels Could Do. Which was quite a lot. The squirrel school had its own sports ground. They had frisbees, basketballs, soccer balls, tennis balls, ping-pong paddles, a rugby ball (which belonged specifically to someone called Louis, and nobody else really understood it), a softball, an eight-ball, and — for reasons that remain unclear — a bullet. They had 125 bows and arrows, 225 sets of scuba diving gear, and they lifted weights every single day. They won everything. Always. Trophies, medals, gold, silver, bronze — all of it. Their cabinets were full. They made films. They drove trains — not ordinary trains, but the Express Cork Train, which moved at the speed of a cheetah. They built castles and roads and whole cities from scratch. They drew maps of everywhere they'd ever been. They launched boats. They launched rockets. They operated satellites from space. They hiked volcanoes and crossed deserts. They camped on islands and beaches — 2,550 camps in total, across every holiday they'd ever taken. Their little villages looked exactly like you'd imagine: small, wooden, and perfectly theirs. They were monster photographers of fireworks. They studied mountains by starlight. They explored the whole world and took pictures of all of it. And for their rockets, they used: trackballs, joysticks, laptops, desktop computers, printers, pagers, projectors, telephones, microphones, mixers, compasses, fire extinguishers, power cables, dead batteries, torches, satellite dishes, and film cameras. Obviously. The End. This is the story of the squirrels who lived on a racetrack. The freezer full of food at the beginning wasn't supposed to be there, but it was, so now it is part of the story forever. No more. There isn't anymore.
